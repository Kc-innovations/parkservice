CHAD BASIN NATIONAL PARK

Chad Basin National Park is a significant protected area in northeastern Nigeria, spanning approximately 2,258 km² across Borno and Yobe States. Established in 1991, the park comprises three distinct sectors:

  • Chingurmi-Duguma Sector (Borno State):
    Features Sudanian savanna with seasonal floodplains, supporting diverse birdlife and mammals.
  • Bade-Nguru Wetlands Sector (Yobe sector):
    Part of the Hadejia-Nguru Wetlands, a Ramsar site, known for migratory birds and aquatic ecosystems.
  • Bulatura Oasis Sector (Yobe State):
    Desert-like dunes and fertile valleys, home to unique desert wildlife.


The park's diverse landscapes include savannas, wetlands, and sand dunes, providing habitats for over 700 plant species and 532 bird species. Notable fauna includes giraffes, ostriches, gazelles, crocodiles, and various bird species like the black-crowned crane and Arabian bustard .

CROSS RIVERS NATIONAL PARK

Cross River National Park is a significant protected area in southeastern Nigeria, renowned for its rich biodiversity and ecological importance. Established in 1991, the park spans approximately 4,000 km² and is divided into two main divisions: Oban and Okwangwo

The park is situated within the Guinea-Congolian rainforest zone, characterized by dense, closed-canopy tropical rainforests. It shares borders with Korup National Park in Cameroon, forming a contiguous ecological zone that enhances its conservation value. The region's climate is tropical, with high annual rainfall contributing to its lush vegetation and diverse ecosystems.


Wildlife and Biodiversity

Cross River National Park is a haven for a variety of wildlife, including several endangered and endemic species:


Primates:
Home to about 78% of Nigeria's primate species, the park hosts the Nigeria-Cameroon chimpanzee, Preuss's red colobus, Sclater's guenon, drill, and the critically endangered Cross River gorilla.

Mammals:
Other notable mammals include forest elephants, leopards, African buffaloes, pangolins, and various rodent species.

Birds:
Over 350 bird species have been recorded, such as the grey-necked rockfowl, bat hawk, and the rare Xavier's greenbul.

Reptiles and Amphibians:
The park is home to 42 snake species and numerous amphibians, contributing to its ecological complexity.

Insects:
Approximately 950 butterfly species have been documented, representing a significant portion of Nigeria's total butterfly diversity.


Divisions of the Park


Oban Division
Location: Situated in the southeastern part of Cross River State, approximately 42 km from Calabar.
Area: Covers about 2,800 km².
Ecology: Characterized by lowland rainforests, rugged terrain with elevations rising above 1,000 meters, and high biodiversity.

Okwangwo Division
Location: Located south-west of the Obudu Plateau, bordering the Takamanda Forest Reserve in Cameroon.
Area: Encompasses approximately 920 km².
Ecology: Features montane grasslands, semi-deciduous forests, and derived savannahs, with elevations reaching up to 1,700 meters.

GASHAKA-GUMTI NATIONAL PARK

Gashaka-Gumti National Park is the largest national park in Nigeria and one of the most ecologically diverse protected areas in West Africa. Here's a detailed overview:

    Overview

  • Location: Northeastern Nigeria, straddling Taraba and Adamawa States.
  • Size: Approximately 6,731 square kilometers (about the size of Delaware in the U.S.).
  • Established: 1991, through the merger of Gashaka and Gumti Game Reserves.
  • Border: Shares a boundary with Cameroon, adjacent to Cameroon’s Faro National Park.

    Ecology and Biodiversity

  • Habitats: Includes lowland forests, montane forests, savannahs, rivers, and grasslands.
  • Biodiversity: Known for its rich flora and fauna, many of which are rare or endangered.

    Notable Wildlife:

  • Primates: Chimpanzees (including the Nigeria-Cameroon chimpanzee, a critically endangered subspecies), baboons, and various monkeys.
  • Large Mammals: Elephants, lions (though rare), leopards, antelope species.
  • Birds: Over 500 species, making it a birdwatcher’s paradise.
  • Insects & Amphibians: A high diversity due to the mix of habitats and elevations.

KAINJI LAKE NATIONAL PARK

Kainji Lake National Park, located in western Nigeria, is one of the country's premier conservation areas. Established in 1979, it encompasses approximately 5,340 square kilometers and is divided into three distinct sectors: the Borgu and Zugurma game reserves, and Kainji Lake itself. The park is situated across Niger and Kwara States and is managed by the Nigerian National Park Service.

Geography and Ecosystems

The park's terrain includes savannah woodlands, riparian forests, and open grasslands. Kainji Lake, formed by the construction of the Kainji Dam on the Niger River, provides a vital aquatic habitat and supports local fishing communities. The Borgu sector is known for its relatively undisturbed wilderness, while the Zugurma sector has faced challenges due to limited accessibility and human encroachment.


Wildlife and Biodiversity

Kainji Lake National Park is home to a diverse array of wildlife. Mammals such as elephants, lions, leopards, hippopotamuses, and various antelope species inhabit the park. It also supports a rich birdlife, including both resident and migratory species. The aquatic ecosystems of Kainji Lake harbor numerous fish species, contributing to the park's biodiversity.


Conservation and Tourism

As Nigeria's first national park, Kainji Lake plays a crucial role in conservation efforts. It serves as a research site for ecological studies and offers opportunities for eco-tourism, including wildlife viewing, birdwatching, and guided tours. However, challenges such as poaching, habitat degradation, and limited infrastructure have impacted its conservation effectiveness.

KAMUKU NATIONAL PARK

Kamuku National Park, situated in Birnin Gwari, Kaduna State, Nigeria, spans approximately 1,120 square kilometers and is characterized by a Sudanian Savanna ecosystem. The park's terrain is predominantly flat, gradually ascending eastwards to the Birnin Gwari Ridge, which forms part of its eastern boundary

Biodiversity and Wildlife

Kamuku National Park boasts a rich biodiversity. It supports about 19 species of mammals, including elephants, roan antelopes, hartebeests, bushbucks, and various primates such as baboons and monkeys. The park is also home to at least 177 bird species, including the secretarybird (Sagittarius serpentarius), Denham's bustard (Neotis denhami), and the Abyssinian ground-hornbill (Bucorvus abyssinicus), which are rare in other parts of Nigeria.


OKOMU NATIONAL PARK

Okomu National Park, located in Edo State, Nigeria, is a significant conservation area preserving one of the country's last remaining lowland rainforests. Established in 1999, the park covers approximately 200 square kilometers within the larger 1,082 km² Okomu Forest Reserve in the Ovia South-West Local Government Area.

Biodiversity and Wildlife

The park is home to a rich variety of flora and fauna. Notably, it shelters the endangered white-throated monkey (Cercopithecus erythrogaster pococki) and the African forest elephant (Loxodonta africana cyclotis). Other species include chimpanzees, red-capped mangabeys, mona monkeys, putty-nosed monkeys, and several antelope species such as Maxwell's duiker, yellow-backed duiker, and red-flanked duiker. The park also boasts a diverse bird population and a variety of butterfly species.


Conservation Efforts

Okomu National Park has faced challenges like illegal logging, poaching, and habitat encroachment. To address these issues, the Africa Nature Investors (ANI) Foundation partnered with the Nigerian National Park Service in 2022 to enhance conservation efforts. This collaboration has led to increased ranger recruitment and training, improved surveillance, and community engagement programs aimed at promoting sustainable livelihoods and reducing reliance on forest resources.

OLD OYO NATIONAL PARK

Old Oyo National Park, located in southwestern Nigeria, spans approximately 2,512 square kilometers across northern Oyo State and southern Kwara State. Established in 1991, the park encompasses the former Upper Ogun and Oyo-Ile forest reserves, which were initially gazetted in 1936 and 1941, respectively, and later merged in 1952 .

Geography and Ecosystems

The park features a diverse landscape of savannah grasslands, woodlands, and forested areas, situated at elevations ranging from 330 to 508 meters above sea level . This varied terrain supports a rich array of flora and fauna, making it a significant ecological zone in Nigeria.


Wildlife and Biodiversity

Old Oyo National Park is home to numerous wildlife species, including African buffalo, kob, bushbuck, roan antelope, western hartebeest, patas monkey, and waterbuck . Although the endangered African wild dog (Lycaon pictus manguensis) once inhabited the park, it has been extirpated due to hunting pressures and human encroachment . The park also boasts a diverse avian population, making it a prime location for birdwatching.


Cultural and Historical Significance

The park holds considerable historical importance, encompassing the ruins of Oyo-Ile, the ancient capital of the Oyo Empire. Visitors can explore remnants of old city walls, moats, and other archaeological sites, offering a glimpse into the region's rich cultural heritage.


Old Oyo National Park stands as a testament to Nigeria's commitment to preserving its natural and cultural heritage, offering a unique blend of ecological diversity and historical richness.
